2. Topological Properties

2.1. Hausdorff Spaces

Definition.     A topological space $X$ is called a Hausdorff space if for each pair $x_1,x_2$ of distinct points of $X$, there exist neighborhoods $U_1$, $U_2$ of $x_1$ and $x_2$ respectively, that are disjoint.

Proposition 2.1.1.    $X$ is a Hausdorff space if for each poin $p$ of $X$, there exists a continuous function $f\colon X\to\mathbb{R}$ such that $f^{-1}(0)={p}$.

Proof.    For each pair $p$, $q$ of distint points of $X$, if there exists a continuous function $f\colon X\to\mathbb{R}$ such that $f^{-1}(0)={p}$, we have $f(p)=0$ and $f(q)=c\neq 0$. Let $\delta=\frac{|c|}{2}>0$, $U=(-\delta,\delta)$, and $V=(c-\delta,c+\delta)$, then $f^{-1}(U)$, $f^{-1}(V)$ are neighborhoods of $p$ and $q$ respectively, and $f^{-1}(U)\cap f^{-1}(V)=\varnothing$. Therefore, $X$ is a Hausdorff space. $\square$

Proposition 2.1.2.    $X$ is a Hausdorff space if and only if the image of the diagonal map $\Delta(X)={(x,x)\colon x\in X}$ is closed in $X\times X$.

Proof.    Since $X$ is a Hausdorff space, for each distinct points $x,y$ of $X$, there exists neighborhoods $U$, $V$ of $x$ and $y$ respectively, and $U\cap V=\varnothing$, then $(U\times V)\cap \Delta(X)=\varnothing$ where $(U\times V)$ is a neighborhood of $(x,y)\in X\times X$, and that means $(\Delta(X))^C$ is open, therefore $\Delta(X)$ is closed. Conversely, if $\Delta(X)$ is closed in $X\times X$, we have $(\Delta(X))^C$ is open, then each $(x,y)\in (\Delta(X))^C$ has a neighborhood with form $U\times V$ where $U$ and $V$ are open in $X$, that is disjoint with $\Delta(X)$, then $U$ and $V$ are neighborhoods of $x$ and $y$ in $X$ respectively, that are disjoint. Therefore, $X$ is a Hausdorff space. $\square$
