Topological Properties

1. The Countability Axioms

First-countability.   A topological space is said to be first-countable if each point has a countable neighbourhood basis.

A neighborhood basis of $x\in X$ is a collection $\mathcal{B}$ of neighborhoods of $x$ such that each neighborhood of $x$ contains at least one of the elements of $\mathcal{B}$. In fact, if $x$ has a countable neighborhood basis $\{U_i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}$, there must be a nonincreasing sequence $V_1\supset V_2\supset V_3\supset\dots$, such that $\{V_i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}$ is also a countable neighborhood basis of $x$, since we can simply let $V_i=\bigcap_{j=1}^i U_j$.

Proposition 1.1   If $X$ is a first-countable space, $A$ is a subset of $X$, then:

  • $x\in\bar{A} \iff$ there exists a sequence of $A$ converges to $x$.
  • $x\in\mathring{A} \iff$ only finite terms of each sequence of $X$ converges to $x$ are belonging to $A^\mathsf{c}$.

Proof.  

  • $(\Leftarrow)$.   If the sequence $\{x_i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}$ of $A$ converges to $x$, then each neighborhood of $x$ intersects $A$ with some terms of $\{x_i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}$ which means $x\in \bar{A}$.
    $(\Rightarrow)$.   Consider a nonincreasing countable neighborhood basis $\mathcal{B}=\{B_i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}$ of $x$, we can find $x_i\in A$ for each $B_i\in\mathcal{B}$ such that $x_i\in B_i$ since $x\in\bar{A}$. From the definition, for each neighborhood $U$ of $x$, $\exists B_N\subset U$, then, for all integers $i>N$, we have $x_i\in B_i\subset B_N\subset U$, thus $\{x_i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}$ converges to $x$.
  • $(\Rightarrow)$.   Directly holds by the definition of convergence since $\mathring{A}$ is a neighborhood of $x$.
    $(\Leftarrow)$.   We assume that $x\not\in\mathring{A}$, then $x\in (\mathring{A})^\mathsf{c}= \overline{A^\mathsf{c}}$, from the previous conclusion, there exists a sequence $\{x_i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}$ of $A^\mathsf{c}$ converges to $x$, which is a contradiction. $\square$

Examples 1.1   The metric spaces are first-countable.

$\{B(x,\frac{1}{n})\}_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ is a countable neighborhood basis of $x$.

Examples 1.2   The Sorgenfrey line $\mathbb{R}_l$ is first-countable.

$\{[x,x+q)\}_{q\in\mathbb{Q}}$ is a countable neighborhood basis of $x$.

Examples 1.3   $\mathbb{R}$ with cocountable topology $\mathcal{T}_c$ is not first-countable.

In fact, if it is, the point $x\in\mathbb{R}$ has a countable neighborhood basis $\{U_i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}$, since $(\bigcap_{i=1}^\infty U_i)^{\mathsf{c}}=\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty U_i^{\mathsf{c}}$ which is a countable set, then $\bigcap_{i=1}^\infty U_i\in\mathcal{T}_c$ and consists of infinitely many elements since $\mathbb{R}$ is uncountable, therefore, we choose $y\in\bigcap_{i=1}^\infty U_i$ such that $y\neq x$, then $(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty U_i)\setminus\{y\}\in\mathcal{T}_c$ and contains no elements of $\{U_i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}$, which is a contradiction.

Second-countability.   A topological space is said to be second-countable if there is a countable basis for its topology.

Separable.   A topological space is said to be separable if it contains a countable, dense subset.

Lindelöf.   A topological space is said to be Lindelöf if every open covering for it contains a countable subcovering.

Proposition 1.2   If $X$ is a second-countable space, then:

  • $X$ is a first-countable space.
  • $X$ is a separable space.
  • $X$ is a Lindelöf space.

Proof.  

  • Let $\mathcal{B}$ be a countable basis of $X$, then for each point $x\in X$, $\{B\in\mathcal{B}\colon\ x\in B\}$ is a countable neighborhood basis of $x$ since each neighborhood of $x$ is an union of elements of $\mathcal{B}$.
  • Let $\mathcal{B}=\{B_i\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}$ be a countable basis of $X$, $A=\{x_i\in X\colon\ i\in\mathbb{N}, x_i\in B_i\}$ which is a countable set. $\forall x\in X$, each neighborhood $U$ of $x$ contains at least one element $B_N\in\mathcal{B}$, then $x_N\in B_N\subset U \implies U\cap A\neq\varnothing$, therefor $\bar{A}=X$, $X$ is separable.
  • Let $\mathcal{B}$ be a countable basis of $X$, $\mathcal{U}$ is an open covering of $X$. We then let $\mathcal{B}’=\{B\in\mathcal{B}\colon\ \exists U\in\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{B}\subset U\}$ and $\mathcal{U}’=\{U_i\in\mathcal{U}\colon\ B_i\in\mathcal{B}, U_i\supset B_i\}$ which are countable. Since $\mathcal{U}$ is an open covering of $X$, for each $x\in X$ there must be a neighborhood $U_x\in\mathcal{U}$ of $x$ and a basis element $B_x$ such that $B_x\subset U_x$, which means $B_x\in\mathcal{B}’$ and therefore $U_x\in\mathcal{U}’$, so $\mathcal{U}’$ is a countable subcovering of $\mathcal{U}$ of $X$, $X$ is Lindelöf. $\square$

Proposition 1.3   If $X$ is a metric space, then:

$X$ is separable $\iff$ $X$ is second-countable $\iff$ $X$ is Lindelöf.

Proof.   It suffices to show that both separability and Lindelöf property imply second-countability, in the metric spaces.

  • If $X$ is separable, there exists a countable subset $A$ such that $\bar{A}=X$. Let $\mathcal{B}=\{B(x,q)\colon\ x\in A, q\in\mathbb{Q}^+\}$, we then prove that $\mathcal{B}$ is a countable basis of $X$.
    For each $x\in X$, each neighborhoods $U$ of $x$ contains a $\delta$-ball: $B(x,\delta)$, since $\bar{A}=X$, there is a point $y\in A$ such that $y\in B(x,\frac{\delta}{3})$, we choose a positive rational number $q$ such that $\frac{\delta}{3}<q<\frac{2\delta}{3}$, then $x\in B(y,q)\subset B(x,\delta)\subset U$, since $\forall z\in B(y,q)$, $B(x,z)\leq B(x,y)+B(y,z)<\frac{\delta}{3}+\frac{2\delta}{3}=\delta$. Therefore, $\mathcal{B}$ is a countable basis of $X$.
  • If $X$ is Lindelöf, let $\mathcal{U}_n=\{B(x,\frac{1}{n})\colon\ x\in X\}$ be a collection of open covering of $X$, then we have countable subcovring $\mathcal{V}_n=\{B(x_i^{(n)},\frac{1}{n})\}_{i\in\mathbb{N}}$ for each $\mathcal{U}_n$. We then prove that $\mathcal{V}=\{B(x_i^{(n)},\frac{1}{n})\colon\ i,n\in\mathbb{N}\}$ is a countable basis of $X$.
    For each $x\in X$, each neighborhoods $U$ of $x$ contains a $\delta$-ball: $B(x,\delta)$. Let $n$ suffitiently large such that $\frac{1}{n}<\delta$, since $\mathcal{V}_{2n}$ is a covering, there exists a point $y$ such that $x\in B(y,\frac{1}{2n})\in \mathcal{V}_{2n}\subset \mathcal{V}$, then $\forall z\in B(y,\frac{1}{2n})$, we have $B(x,z)\leq B(x,y)+B(y,z)<\frac{1}{2n}+\frac{1}{2n}<\delta \implies x\in B(y,\frac{1}{2n})\subset B(x,\delta)\subset U$, which means $\mathcal{V}$ is a countable basis of $X$. $\square$
